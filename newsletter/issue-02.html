Get more cooling from the AC you already own

Late July is when Cape Cod cooling bills peak — and when we hear the most "my AC runs all day and the bedroom still won't cool." Before anyone talks new equipment, it's worth asking what energy efficiency means for your house. Usually the answer starts with five things you can check this weekend.

Start with the cheap stuff

Check the filter monthly in cooling season. A clogged filter starves the system of airflow, so the coil runs colder than designed, can ice over, and delivers less cool air while using more electricity. Hold it up to a light — if you can't see light through it, replace it.

Pick a temperature and hold it. Setting the thermostat lower doesn't cool the house faster; the system runs at one speed either way. It just overshoots. Choose the warmest setting you're comfortable at, because every degree you don't ask for is runtime you don't pay for.

Use fans to raise that setting. Ceiling fans spinning counterclockwise push air down and cool your skin, not the room — so you can hold the thermostat a bit higher and feel the same. Turn fans off in empty rooms; they add heat, not cooling.

Work with Cape Cod nights

Flush the house after dark. Our evenings usually cool off fast. Open windows on opposite sides of the house, run a window or bath fan to pull air through, then close everything at sunrise to trap the cool. Your AC starts the day with a head start.

Look at the ducts. Ducts running through a hot attic or crawlspace leak cooled air where it does you no good. Seal accessible joints with mastic or foil tape — never cloth duct tape, which dries out. From the Field
✓ Keep shrubs and grass clippings two feet clear of the outdoor unit — it needs to breathe to shed heat.
✓ Close blinds on south- and west-facing windows by noon; sun through glass is free heat you then pay to remove.
✓ Run the dishwasher, dryer, and oven after dark so their heat lands when the AC isn't fighting the sun.
