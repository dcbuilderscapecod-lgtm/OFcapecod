Hot water: the energy line nobody watches

Late August on the Cape means outdoor showers, guest towels in every load, and a water heater running flat out. It sits in the basement doing its job so quietly that most homeowners never think about it — which is exactly why it's worth ten minutes of your attention this week.

Start at the dial

After heating and cooling, making hot water is typically the biggest energy job in a house. First check: the thermostat on the tank. Many are set to 140°F from the factory; 120°F is plenty for showers and laundry, cuts standby heat loss, and lowers scald risk. Modern dishwashers boost their own water temperature, so they don't need the tank running hot.

Second check: put your hand on the tank. If it feels warm, it's shedding heat around the clock, and an insulation blanket plus pipe wrap on the first few feet of hot line is one of the cheapest fixes in the house. Older electric tanks benefit most.

When the tank is getting old

A standard tank lasts roughly 8–12 years. If yours is in that window, plan the replacement before it fails — an emergency swap means taking whatever's on the truck. That's the moment to consider a heat pump water heater: it pulls heat from the surrounding air instead of making it, and as a bonus it dehumidifies the space around it — genuinely useful in a Cape Cod basement. It needs some air volume around it and a condensate drain, so placement matters.

  • Fixture flow: a quality low-flow showerhead and faucet aerators cut hot water use without anyone noticing.

From the Field
✓ Write the install year on your tank with a marker — future you will thank you.
✓ Test the dial change for a week; if nobody notices, 120°F was always enough.
✓ Hear popping or rumbling from the tank? That's sediment — worth a look before winter.
