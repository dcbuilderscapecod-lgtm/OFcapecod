The end-of-summer exterior walkaround

Late August is the last easy window to inspect your home's exterior: the wood is dry, the light is long, and caulk and paint still cure well. Twenty minutes with a notepad now beats discovering a leak in a February nor'easter — and every gap you close is heat you keep.

Walk it the way a builder does

Your exterior is one system. Siding sheds water, caulk seals the joints, gutters carry it away, and grade moves it off the foundation. A failure in any one link lets water and outside air into the wall — and air leaks are the cheapest energy problem to fix, because your heating system spends all winter replacing whatever escapes. Circle the house slowly, sun at your back, and look for these:

  • Siding and trim: press suspect boards with a screwdriver handle. Soft or spongy means rot behind the paint. Check corner boards and anywhere a roof dumps onto a wall first.
  • Caulk lines: scan around windows, doors, and where trim meets siding. Cracked or pulled-away caulk is both a water entry and an air leak. If a bead has gone hard and split, cut it out and recaulk — don't bury it under a new layer.
  • Gutters and downspouts: water should exit at least a few feet from the foundation. Staining or moss on siding below a gutter means it's overflowing.
  • Grade: soil should slope away from the house on all sides. Mulch piled against shingles or siding wicks moisture straight into the wood.

The energy angle

Ask yourself where your house feels drafty by Halloween — that's usually where the caulk failed. For what a full envelope fix involves, see our siding, trim, and repair services or browse the blog.

From the Field
✓ Photograph anything questionable — comparing the same spot next spring tells you if it's moving.
✓ Caulk cures best between roughly 40 and 80 degrees, so early fall is prime recaulking season on the Cape.
✓ A garden hose run over one roof or wall section at a time finds leaks faster than waiting for rain.
