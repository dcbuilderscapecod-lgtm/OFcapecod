Heat Pumps, Explained Like a Neighbor Would

September is the sweet spot: the heating season hasn't started, contractors' calendars haven't filled, and you have time to think instead of react. If you've been hearing about heat pumps and wondering what they'd actually mean for your house and your bills, here's the short, honest version.

A heat pump doesn't burn anything — it moves heat. A refrigerant loop pulls warmth out of outdoor air and carries it inside, the same physics as your refrigerator run in reverse. Even cold winter air holds heat energy, and today's cold-climate models are built to keep extracting it through a Cape Cod January. In July the cycle flips, and the same unit becomes your air conditioning.

Ducted or mini-split?

  • Ducted: one outdoor unit feeding your ductwork — one thermostat, whole-house comfort, the natural fit if you already have forced hot air.
  • Mini-splits: no ducts needed — a slim wall or ceiling unit in each zone, each with its own control. The usual answer for older Capes with baseboard heat, additions, and that room over the garage that's never comfortable.

What changes on the bill

Your electric bill goes up while oil or propane deliveries shrink or stop — one number grows so the other can fade. How that nets out depends on your rates, your insulation, and how you run the system, which is why we tell people to air-seal and insulate first: a tighter house needs a smaller system and runs it less. You don't have to rip anything out, either — many Cape homeowners keep the existing boiler or furnace as backup for the coldest snaps, letting each system do what it does best. Mass Save offers no-cost home energy assessments, and rebates change year to year, so check their current offerings before you commit.

From the Field
✓ Insulate and air-seal before you size a heat pump — a tighter house needs smaller equipment and runs it less.
✓ Mount the outdoor unit above the snow line and keep shrubs clear so airflow never chokes.
✓ Pick a comfortable temperature and leave it — heat pumps run best steady, not with big nightly setbacks.
