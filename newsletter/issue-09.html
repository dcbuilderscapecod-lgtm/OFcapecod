Find the Air Leaks Before Heating Season

The first cold night on the Cape usually shows up before the calendar says fall. Air sealing is the cheapest comfort upgrade a house can get, and most of it is a Saturday-morning job. This week: where your house actually leaks, how to find the leaks yourself, and which sealant belongs where.

Where houses leak (hint: mostly not the windows)

Warm air rises and escapes through the top of the house; that pull drags cold air in at the bottom. Builders call it the stack effect, and it means the biggest leaks live high and low, not at eye level:

  • Rim joist — the band of framing that sits on the foundation. In many older Cape homes it’s uninsulated and gappy where the sill meets concrete.
  • Attic hatch — often a bare piece of plywood with no weatherstripping; it can leak like a partly open window.
  • Penetrations — plumbing, wiring, bath fans, and flue chases that pass through the attic floor or basement ceiling.

Find them with a stick of incense

Pick a windy day, or run the kitchen and bath fans to pull air out of the house. Hold a lit incense stick near each suspect spot: if the smoke ribbon wavers or gets sucked sideways, you’ve found a leak. Mark it with painter’s tape and keep moving.

Which sealant goes where

  • Caulk for gaps under about a quarter inch between parts that don’t move.
  • Canned foam for bigger, irregular holes like pipe penetrations — fire-rated foam near flues and chimneys.
  • Weatherstrip for anything that opens: doors, windows, that attic hatch.

And if the incense test points to something bigger — a chronically cold room, ice dams, moisture in the basement — that’s a building problem, not a caulk problem. See our construction services or request a consultation.

From the Field
✓ A dryer vent with a broken flapper leaks all winter — the damper should close flat when the dryer is off.
✓ Feel outlets on exterior walls on a cold morning; foam gaskets behind the cover plates take five minutes to install.
✓ Seal the attic before adding insulation — insulation slows heat, but it doesn’t stop moving air.
