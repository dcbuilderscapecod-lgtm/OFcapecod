Insulation: why the attic comes first

The first cool nights just hit the Cape, which makes September the right month to think about where your heat actually goes. Before the furnace runs daily, one question is worth an hour in the attic: is the top of your house doing its job? Here's how to know.

Heat rises, and it pulls cold air in behind it

Warm air is buoyant. In winter it climbs to the top of your house and leaks out through gaps in the attic floor — around recessed lights, plumbing stacks, the chimney chase, the hatch. As it escapes, it pulls cold outside air in low, through the basement and sills. That loop is the stack effect, and it runs all heating season. Slow it at the top and the whole loop slows — which is why the attic comes before walls or windows.

R-value, in plain words

R-value measures how well a material resists heat flow: higher number, slower heat loss. Two things quietly ruin it — compression (insulation squashed under stored boxes loses much of its rating) and gaps (even a small void lets heat pour through). Even depth and full coverage beat any brand name.

Seal first, then insulate

Fiberglass and cellulose slow heat, but moving air carries heat straight through them. Air-seal the attic floor penetrations before adding a single new inch, or the new layer just filters the breeze. Common Cape attic mistakes we find:

  • Insulation stuffed into soffit vents — blocks roof airflow, invites moisture and ice dams
  • Bath fans venting into the attic instead of outdoors — wet framing and mold
  • An uninsulated, unweatherstripped hatch — a big hole in an otherwise good layer
  • Old recessed lights left unsealed — little chimneys for warm air

From the Field
✓ If your attic floor joists are visible above the insulation, you almost certainly need more depth.
✓ Feel around the attic hatch on a cold morning — a draft there means warm air is escaping all day.
✓ Check that every bath fan duct runs to a roof or gable vent, not just into the attic space.
