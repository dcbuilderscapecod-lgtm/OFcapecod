Storm-ready: prepping your Cape home for nor'easter season

Noreaster season on the Cape runs roughly October through April, and the first big blow usually finds whatever you didn't get to. The good news: most storm damage starts with small, checkable things. Here's the walk-around we'd do at our own houses this month — an hour outside now saves a midnight phone call later.

Start where the wind starts: trees and limbs

Walk the yard and look up. Any limb overhanging the roof, deck, or service line is a candidate to come down in a 50-mph gust. Dead wood shows itself now — bare branches while the rest of the tree still holds leaves. Prune what you can reach from the ground; hire an arborist for anything over a ladder's height or near wires. Never touch a limb resting on a power line.

Water goes where you let it

  • Sump pump: pour a bucket of water into the pit. If the float doesn't kick the pump on within seconds, service it now — and remember it dies with the power unless it has battery backup.
  • Gutters and downspouts: clear them, then confirm downspouts discharge at least a few feet from the foundation. Pooling at the sill is how basements flood and framing rots.
  • Garage door: it's the biggest opening in your house. Check that the tracks are tight, rollers move freely, and the bottom seal actually seals — a failed garage door lets wind pressurize the whole structure.

Power and people

Run a portable generator outdoors only, at least 20 feet from the house, exhaust pointed away — never in a garage, even with the door open. Test it under load before the storm, not during. From the Field
✓ Photograph your roof, siding, and deck now — before-and-after photos make insurance claims far easier.
✓ Charge every drill and headlamp battery when a storm is 48 hours out; they double as phone chargers and light.
✓ If shingles lift at the edges when you press them by hand, the sealant strip has failed — get eyes on the roof before winter.
