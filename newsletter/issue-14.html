Wood stoves and fireplaces: cozy without the energy penalty

First fires of the season are lighting up across Falmouth, Mashpee, and Bourne. Here's the catch: an open fireplace can pull more heat out of your house than it puts in. A few smart moves keep the crackle and the comfort — without your furnace working overtime to make up the difference.

A fire needs air, and an open fireplace takes it from the room — air your heating system already paid to warm — and sends it up the flue. Worse, a warm chimney keeps drafting for hours after the flames die, siphoning heated air all night. That's the energy penalty, and every piece of it is fixable.

Start with the damper

  • Check the seal. Old throat dampers warp and rust; many never close fully, leaking warm air all winter even when you're not burning.
  • Consider a top-sealing damper. Mounted at the chimney cap with a gasket, it seals far tighter and keeps rain and animals out too.
  • Add glass doors. They slow room-air loss as the fire burns down, so you can go to bed without leaving a hole in the house open.

Inserts, stoves, and outside air

An EPA-certified wood insert or freestanding stove turns a fireplace from decoration into a real heater: a closed firebox burns the wood more completely, and a blower pushes heat into the room instead of up the chimney. Pair it with an outside combustion air kit, which feeds the fire directly so it stops depressurizing the house and dragging cold air in through every gap. And keep up the basics — an annual chimney sweep and inspection protects both efficiency and safety.

From the Field
✓ Dollar-bill test: close the damper on a bill — if it slides out with no resistance, the seal is leaking heat.
✓ Burn only seasoned, dry hardwood; wet wood wastes heat boiling off water and coats your flue with creosote.
✓ Feel a cold draft at an unused fireplace? A chimney balloon is a quick fix until you upgrade the damper.
