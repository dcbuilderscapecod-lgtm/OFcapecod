Drafty doors: weekend fixes that actually work

First cold snap of the season and suddenly you can feel the front hall from the couch. A drafty door is one of the cheapest energy problems on Cape Cod to fix yourself — usually an hour, a screwdriver, and a trip to the hardware store. Here's what actually works.

Find the leak first

On a windy night, hold a lit incense stick or a strip of tissue around the door edges. Flame flickers and paper flutters right where air moves. Most leaks show up in two places: under the door and along the latch side, where hinges have sagged the slab out of square.

The bottom: sweep or threshold

  • A door sweep screws to the inside face and takes ten minutes. Get one with a vinyl or silicone fin, not brush bristles — brushes let wind through.
  • Many exterior doors have an adjustable threshold: four or five screws along the sill raise or lower it. Turn them a quarter-turn at a time until a dollar bill drags when you pull it out under the closed door.

The sides and top: weatherstrip

If you can see daylight, replace the weatherstrip. Kerf-style (the kind that presses into a slot in the frame) seals best and pulls out by hand for replacement. Foam tape is the cheap fallback — it works for a season, then compresses flat. Match the old profile; a fatter strip can keep the door from latching.

When the door is the problem

If the gap is wider at the top than the bottom, the door has sagged or the frame has shifted — common in older Cape homes. Tighten hinge screws first; if that fails, no weatherstrip will save it. That's a repair or replacement call, and it's the kind of moisture and repair work we handle year-round. More seasonal fixes on our blog.

From the Field
✓ Close the door on a strip of paper at the top, latch side, and sill — anywhere it slides free leaks.
✓ Tighten hinge screws before buying anything; a sagged door defeats every seal.
✓ Replace one weatherstrip piece at a time so you can match the profile at the store.
