The No-Cost Energy Assessment, Explained

Heating season is here on the Cape, and November is when drafty rooms and busy furnaces make themselves known. Before you spend a dime on upgrades, there's a smarter first step: a no-cost Mass Save home energy assessment. Here's what actually happens when the energy specialist shows up.

Booking is the easy part

Call Mass Save or sign up on their website and pick a date. Most Massachusetts homeowners qualify through their electric or gas utility, and the assessment itself costs nothing. Set aside an hour or two, and make sure the attic hatch, basement, and mechanical room are reachable.

What the specialist actually checks

  • Insulation: attic, walls, and basement — where it's thin, missing, or compressed.
  • Air leaks: around attic hatches, recessed lights, rim joists, and plumbing penetrations — the hidden gaps that matter more than window drafts.
  • Heating and hot water: age, condition, and efficiency of your furnace, boiler, or heat pump and water heater.
  • Same-day items: many visits include installing efficient bulbs, low-flow showerheads, and thermostat checks on the spot.

You leave with a written report ranking upgrades — air sealing and insulation usually top the list because they cost the least and touch every room. Rebates and incentives change regularly, so check Mass Save's current offerings rather than relying on last year's numbers.

Then ask yourself the real question

What does saving energy mean in your house? A bedroom that finally stays warm? Lower bills? Fewer ice dams? Your answer decides which recommendations to act on first. If the report flags work you'd fold into a bigger project — an addition, new siding, a remodel — that's the moment to plan them together, since open walls are the cheapest time to insulate. From the Field
✓ Photograph your attic and basement insulation before the visit — the specialist will want to see both anyway.
✓ Clear a path to the water heater, furnace, and attic hatch so nothing gets skipped.
✓ Ask for the report in writing and keep it — it's your upgrade roadmap for the next few years.
