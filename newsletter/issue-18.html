Getting the house ready for holiday hosting

A houseful of guests is the toughest test your home faces all year — more showers, more cooking, more door-openings, and rooms in use that usually sit empty. Here's how to get ahead of it, and how to tell a ten-minute fix from a project worth booking for the new year.

Hot water: know your recovery time

Every water heater has a recovery rate — how fast it reheats after a big draw. A standard tank needs a stretch between back-to-back showers; a heat pump water heater in efficiency mode needs longer. Two moves before company arrives: confirm the thermostat sits around 120°F (hotter wastes energy and risks scalding), and switch a heat pump unit to hybrid or high-demand mode for the week. Stagger showers around dishwasher and laundry loads — they all pull from the same tank.

Comfort and moisture with a crowd

  • Bath fans: six people showering puts gallons of water vapor into the air. Run the fan 20 minutes after every shower. If the mirror stays fogged, the fan is undersized or the duct is blocked — check that the exterior flap actually opens.
  • Guest rooms: open closed-off rooms a day early so walls and furniture warm up, and make sure baseboards and registers aren't blocked by beds pushed against them.
  • Drafts: the door everyone uses leaks the most. Fresh weatherstripping is a ten-minute job that guests will feel.

Quick fix or new-year project?

If a room never warms up, hot water runs out nightly, or a bathroom grows mildew every winter, that's not a holiday problem — it's insulation, ventilation, or an undersized system. From the Field
✓ Set the water heater to about 120°F — hot enough for a full house, cool enough to save energy and prevent scalding.
✓ A working bath fan will hold a square of toilet paper against the grille; if it drops, clean the fan or check the duct.
✓ Test guest-room heat a week out, not the night before — a cold zone takes time to diagnose.
