Tight house, fresh air: ventilation basics

The windows are shut for the season, and if you've been air-sealing and insulating along with us this fall, your house is holding heat better than ever. Here's the flip side: a tight house no longer breathes by accident. This week — how to give it fresh air on purpose.

Why tight homes need a plan for air

Old, drafty houses swap air through every crack — you pay to heat that leakage, but it does carry moisture and stale air out. Seal those cracks and the moisture from cooking, showers, laundry, and breathing stays inside. The rule builders use: build tight, ventilate right. You control where air comes in and goes out, instead of letting the walls do it.

Three layers of ventilation

  • Spot exhaust: a bath fan that vents outside (not into the attic — check this) and runs 20 minutes after every shower. A timer switch makes it automatic. Kitchen range hoods should also duct outdoors, not just recirculate.
  • Whole-house fresh air: very tight homes need continuous low-level ventilation, not just spot fans.
  • ERV/HRV: the smart version. These units push stale air out and pull fresh air in through a core that transfers heat between the two streams — so winter fresh air arrives pre-warmed by the air leaving. An ERV also moderates humidity. This is standard equipment in the high-performance homes we build.

Moisture signals your house is asking for help

Condensation pooling on window sills, a bathroom mirror that stays fogged long after a shower, musty closets on exterior walls, or black spotting in cold corners — all signs moisture is being made faster than it's leaving. Caught early, these are ventilation fixes, not rot repairs. More on our energy-smart building services, and past issues on air-sealing live on the blog.

From the Field
✓ Run the bath fan 20 minutes after every shower — a cheap timer switch makes it automatic.
✓ Check where your bath fan duct ends: it should exit through the roof or wall, never dump into the attic.
✓ Crack interior doors or undercut them so bedrooms don't go stale when doors are closed all night.
