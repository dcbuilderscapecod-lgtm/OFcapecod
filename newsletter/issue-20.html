Ice dams: prevent them now, not in February

Every February we get calls about water stains spreading across ceilings after a snowstorm. By then the only options are emergency ones. The real fix happens now, in early winter, before the first big snow sits on your roof — and it starts with asking where your heat is going.

Why ice dams form

An ice dam is a heat-loss problem wearing a roof-problem costume. Warm air escapes your living space into the attic, warms the roof deck, and melts the snow from underneath. That meltwater runs down to the cold overhang at the eave — where there's no heated space below — and refreezes into a ridge of ice. New meltwater pools behind the ridge and works its way under the shingles. Shingles shed water; they don't hold it back when it's standing.

The cure is in the attic

  • Air seal first. Warm air rides through gaps around attic hatches, recessed lights, bath fans, chimneys, and top plates. Sealing these leaks does more than insulation alone.
  • Then insulate. Deep, even coverage keeps the roof deck cold so snow melts from sunshine, not from your heating bill. Thin spots show up as bare streaks on a snowy roof.
  • Then ventilate. Soffit-to-ridge airflow flushes out whatever heat still sneaks through, keeping the deck near outdoor temperature.

Gutters don't cause ice dams and cleaning them won't prevent one — but clogged gutters give that first refreeze a head start, so clear them anyway. Heat cables treat the symptom, not the source.

Notice the pattern: everything that stops ice dams also cuts your heating load. A Mass Save home energy assessment is no-cost and will flag air leaks and thin insulation — rebate offerings change, so check what's current. Or read more on our blog and our energy-smart building services.

From the Field
