Smart Thermostats: Set Up Right or Don't Bother

December is when your heating system earns its keep, and a smart thermostat left on factory settings is just an expensive clock. Ten minutes of honest setup decides whether it actually trims your bill. Here's how we'd set one up — and the rule that changes completely if you heat with a heat pump.

The schedule is the whole game

A smart thermostat saves energy one way: holding a lower temperature during hours you don't need heat. So program your real week, not the default one. Ask yourself:

  • When is the house actually empty? If someone works from home, an away setback may barely exist — and that's fine.
  • When are you asleep? A cooler bedroom overnight is the easiest win in most homes.
  • Weekends differ from weekdays. Program them separately or the schedule fights you, and you'll override it until it's useless.

Heat pumps and boilers play by different rules

With a boiler or furnace, a deep overnight setback generally saves fuel — the system recovers quickly in the morning. A heat pump is different: it's most efficient running steadily, and a deep setback can trigger the electric resistance backup during recovery, which can wipe out what the setback saved. With a heat pump, keep setbacks modest — a few degrees — or hold one steady temperature, and make sure the thermostat is configured for your exact equipment so it knows when to lock out that backup heat.

Placement decides what the thermostat "sees"

Mount it on an interior wall away from direct sun, drafts, the kitchen, and heat-throwing electronics. If your thermostat supports remote sensors, put them in the rooms you actually live in — not the hallway.
